Overview

Is there an innate human limits to the ability of human groups to define problems and/or discover solutions with regards to the Sustainable Development Goals or SDGs? By virtue of being innate, any such human limits to the ability of human groups to define problems and/or discover solutions are posited to be independent of any SDGs related theory or methodology, regardless of whether those theories and/or methods be the latest and the state-of-the-art. If so such limits appear to be almost completely unknown.

The presentation of the topic in the mini-symposium's overview is formulated as a question, which invites participants to explore and debate the issue rather than asserting it as a definitive statement. The questions posed are expected to open up debate, encourage diverse viewpoints, and lead to a deeper understanding of the subject matter. This approach is aimed at fostering a deeper examination of the complexities and challenges involved in problem-solving in economics and the role of collective intelligence in navigating these challenges.

However, the audience is also invited to contribute unknown or lesser-known historical examples and case studies, that can be a more effective approach for a symposium aimed at exploring uncharted territories of strong collective intelligence (CI) and the SDGs. This approach aligns with the exploratory nature of the symposium, fostering a more dynamic and inclusive environment where participants can share and discover novel insights, perspectives, and information that may not be widely recognized or established.

A potential practical experience of such limits, though it might not be recognized as such, is that in efforts to address complex societal challenges, whether poverty, sustainable economic development, as well as access to affordable healthcare, education, energy, or other resources, a common theme often appears. This common theme is the current inability of human groups to design decision-making systems that reliably converge on the solutions with the greatest actual or projected fitness in terms of their impact on collectively targeted outcomes. The realization that donors and other stakeholders are at times reliably unable to discover the value in much better solutions, has in recent years spurred research in decision-making and intelligence to try to discover what is missing.

As a specific case, current decision-making processes that groups of stakeholders use for selecting interventions meant to optimize collective outcomes, such as for societal challenges like the SDGs, are in some cases unable to reliably discover and select the complex networks of solutions (see the example of the School Uniform Value Chain) that are capable of vastly increasing impact on the problems to the point that solutions might be reliably achievable. They might not even have a model for what collectively optimal even means (see the case of General Collective Optimization as applied to poverty as an example). Other cases where groups of stakeholders are unable to reliably discover solutions are wherever the correct perspective is conflated with the consensus perspective, and where the consensus is determined by a group the user is not part of and that is misaligned with the users objectives.

As another example, one error due to lack of collective intelligence is assuming that solutions will not come from those whom the consensus do not deem to be experts. This assumption can lead to over-valuing expert opinions that are based on the current rather than the future state of knowledge. It can also lead to a misalignment with solving problems in a way that is collectively optimal, particularly where solutions might be visibly or invisibly constrained to be aligned with various corporate or other interests that are assumed to be the seat of that expertise.

Symposium Focus Areas

This symposium on the limits to the general problem-solving ability of human groups (limits to their problem-solving ability that potentially apply to any problem at all) due to their lack of strong CI is focused on approaches that might be explored in the future towards solving the problem of surpassing any strong CI related limits to addressing the SDGs. This discussion is open to researchers in any discipline.

Background

Workshop participants may find the following content useful:

Significantly increasing positive impact on the SDGs, or even solving them isnt just a matter of coming up with a better idea or more funding. Because in some cases, stakeholders involved in funding and/or implementing such interventions lack the capacity to reliably discover and/or select those significantly better interventions. Simply put, they might lack the metrics to make this difference clear, and they might lack the capacity to reliably make the comparison even if they did, as opposed to simply ignoring solutions outside of the consensus. All of these point to the potential existence of innate limits to the problem-solving ability of human groups with respect to the SDGs.

Having discovered patterns of interventions that in some cases predicted significant increases in impact on the SDGs, the problem became figuring out why groups were reliably unable to appreciate these solutions. In some cases these predicted increases were game-changing, rising to the point that such solutions might become sustainably self-funding and actually solve the problems by enabling impact globally at the scale at which it is required, rather than just at the scale at which donor funding is available.

Hypothetical limits to the problem-solving abilities of human groups were then obtained by working backwards to try to remove specific instances of problems concerning the inability of stakeholders to detect where such solutions were predicted to massively increase impact on complex societal challenges like poverty, sustainable economic development, access to affordable healthcare, education, energy, etc.

The literature reflects some research on the limits to the ability of human groups to solve the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). For instance, the "17 Rooms" initiative, as described by the Brookings Institution, is an innovative approach to problem-solving across the SDGs. In addition, the World Resources Institute outlines four key challenges to achieving the SDGs: involving the whole government, engaging all sectors of society, securing buy-in by financial institutions, and making stakeholders accountable. But neither of these makes the connection between these limits and the lack of strong CI.

A proposed ten phase Collective Intelligence based Program to Accelerate Achievement of the Sustainable Development Goals (CIPAA-SDGs) is intended to leverage networks of cooperating interventions to create vastly greater impact per program dollar on targeted goals related to the SDGs. Where the SDGs have not yet proven reliably solvable today, the CIPAA-SDGs program aims to use collective intelligence to increase impact to the point that such impact might become sustainably self-funding once launched, and impact might be achieved at the scale at which it is required, rather than just the scale at which funding is available, with the goal of making solutions to such challenges reliably achievable.

To build support for this program, a proposed Change the World Global University Challenge aims to offer a prize for the solution that leverages patterns of collective intelligence to achieve the greatest predicted impact on the sectors within the focus of each phase.

In order to predict and compare these impacts at a high level, the contest aims provide tools that allow this impact on the SDGs to be simulated and compared. In addition to creating awareness of the tremendous potential to increase impact on the SDGs through strong CI, the goal of this challenge is also to use the simulations to assess the feasiblity programs that aim to leverage strong CI based funding initiatives (initiatives that leverage network effects directed at significantly increasing funding for the SDGs). These initiatives are designed with the goal of reliably driving as much as $30 billion USD towards achieving the goals of the program in its initial deployment regions of East Africa and the Caribbean, so that such instruments might be replicated globally.

But human problem-solving doesnt just reside in the domain of cognition, and therefore some of the potential reasons groups are limited in their problem-solving ability are behavioral and require self-awareness on the part of the problem-solver of their own behavior. Other behavior related reasons require awareness of the audience.

As an example, the collective social brain hypothesis posits that humans strongly tend to fall into two groups or two halves of the collective social brain. One that sees themselves as vulnerable and that prioritizes rights and protections using system I (fast or intuitive) reasoning to assess consensus, and one that prioritizes individual responsibility using system II (rational methodical) that can be evaluated independently of any consensus at all. These halves use different reasoning strategies and come up with at times irreconcilable answers given the same information.

But both strategies and the ability to switch between them are required for groups to have general or strong CI. Consensus and intuition are useful or necessary when solutions are not computable, which might be the case where a suitable algorithm doesnt exist, where the required inputs are not available, or where the computing capacity (ability to execute the algorithm) is insufficient. Rational logic is useful or necessary when solutions are in fact computable.

Another behavior related limit to problem-solving that requires awareness of the audience is what speaker Simon Sinek calls the "law of diffusion of innovation". According to Sinek, due to this law the most effective way for the 2.5% of individuals who are innovators to solve the problem of introducing a disruptive new idea, is to target the 13.5% of the population who are early adopters looking for ideas rather than evidence, and invite them explore the ideas and help find the empirical evidence required to convince everyone else. These early adopters, Sinek says, are most effectively targeted by answering the question "why?". In other words, it's most important to find the people who are motivated by the same "why", that is, who the people believe what the problem-solver believes. Derek Sivers, another Ted Talk speaker, made the point that in launching a new movement, it's critically important to nurture the first few followers. In a conference this might mean giving them a chance to be heard and celebrating their participation. However the most important lesson according to Sivers is that the first followers might even be more important than the leader themselves. Because its the first followers who transform an unknown visionary problem-solver into a leader. The takeaway of Sivers was then that if one really cares about solving the problem of starting a movement, its important to inspire the courage to follow and show others how to follow, and when one finds a lone visionary doing something great, it's important to have the courage to stand up and join in.

The implications of these strong CI related limits on the problem solving ability of human groups, are profound. If such limits to the complexity that human groups can navigate without strong CI exist, then the same methods that might be reliably successful within these limits of complexity, might reliably be unsuccessful outside them.
