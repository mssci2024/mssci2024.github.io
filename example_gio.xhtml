Example of General Collective Optimization

The design of a “Social Impact Marketplace” platform is a proposed implementation of a specific limited subset of GCI functionality. Where networks of cooperating interventions have the potential to achieve exponentially greater impact on collective outcomes than any stand-alone intervention competing with other interventions for impact on its own, this platform aims to enable a group of users of different roles to reliably converge on selecting the network of interventions that is most fit in optimizing a collectively targeted outcome. The goal is to increase collective impact to the point it might become self-sustaining with the inputs that are available in the local environment, so that social impact might be achieved at vastly greater scale.

 

Specifically, a collective outcome is defined here as reflecting the outcome achieved by each member of the group. As an example, when optimizing collective salaries, it’s important to differentiate between the case in which 1 person in a group of 1000 earns $100,000,000 USD and the rest earn $0 USD, and the case in which all 1000 people each earn $100,000 USD, both of which scenarios result in an average salary of $100,000 USD, but the latter of which likely results in each individual being far more fit to take care of their needs. Ignoring any network effects within the group, we define the collective salary outcome for N people as:

 

Salary Outcome COLLECTIVE = Φ ( Salary i ) ⋅ ∑ i = 1 N Salary i
 

Where Φ(Salaryi) is a non-linear function of Salaryi (the salary of person “i”) which reflects the theory that increased salary has limited benefit on individual well-being once it significantly surpasses the level at which basic needs are met.

 

The function Φ(Salaryi) can be seen as a function that achieves General Individual Optimization or GIO, in this case as a function of salary.

 

A commonly-used function to reflect the diminishing returns of income on well-being is the logarithm function, specifically the natural logarithm. This is based on ample empirical evidence from the fields of economics and psychology which suggests that the marginal utility of income decreases as income increases.

 

Φ ( Salary i ) = a ⋅ ln ( Salary i + b ) + c
 

For illustrative purposes, We assume a=1, b=10,000, and c=0. The implication of this is that each unit increase in the log of income corresponds to a one-unit increase in well-being. This assumption results in a moderate rate of diminishing returns on well-being as income increases. We assume the first $10,000 of income does not significantly impact the logarithm output (given that we're adding it inside the log function) but ensures that the function is defined even for a salary of $0. This could imply a context where the first $10,000 is considered vital for meeting basic needs and therefore does not contribute as much to the diminishing returns effect. Beyond this level, additional income contributes to well-being but at a decreasing rate. We assume a zero baseline for well-being when the logarithm output is zero.

 

The implication is that if the salary is equal to $-b (i.e., -$10,000, which is not physically meaningful but serves to ground our function), the well-being (or the output of Φ) is zero. For positive salaries, the well-being increases but at a diminishing rate due to the logarithmic relationship.

One way of achieving optimal collective outcomes with regards to salaries is to design and deploy a decentralized network of local businesses that cooperate to gain potentially unbeatable competitive advantage for local products and services created with local labor. The Social Impact Marketplace allows all roles in the marketplace (donors, entrepreneurs, lenders, investors, etc.) to cooperate to select an entire value chain that they might then cooperate to implement, where the algorithm is designed to converge on selecting the intervention with optimal collective impact.

 

Including these networks, the collective salary outcome changes to:

 

Salary Outcome COLLECTIVE = Φ ( Salary ⃗ )
 
Where Salary ⃗  is a vector in which each element at position “i” is Salaryi. The function Φ ( Salary ⃗ )  can be seen as a function that achieves General Collective Optimization or GCO, in this case as a function of salary.

 

But implementing a small subset of GCI only allows GCO to be deployed in a limited problem domain. No network of GCI based initiatives is assured to have the capacity to gain sufficient competitive advantage on its own to implement the entire set of GCI functionality as the cost and complexity of implementing GCI rises. A hierarchy of network effects must be created between network based initiatives in order to increase capacity to pay this cost until it can reliably be afforded. To use biological systems as a metaphor, a circulatory system that makes cells more efficient at exchanging resources and metabolic wastes with the environment, or a cognitive system that increases the power of cells to make abstract representations of their environments, or any other system, can’t be deployed on its own if those systems achieve these benefits at costs that are unsustainable. These outcomes must be achieved within networks of other systems that combine to make the costs sustainable instead.

 

In this way, systemic change has to be coordinated. In proposing a ten phase Collective Intelligence based Program to Accelerate Achievement of the Sustainable Development Goals (CIPAA-SDGs), part of which involves deploying a collective intelligence based smart city, the goal was to facilitate the self-assembly of massive cooperation directed at recreating essential services that underlie a community, but recreating those elements based on GCO so that their natural balance would be for current problems resulting from GIO to be fixed. And the goal was to coordinate the deployment of all of these components across the phases of the CIPAA-SDGs program. This CIPAA-SDGs is designed to deploy entire value chains of businesses leveraging collectively intelligent cooperation to ensure market success of such initiatives.
